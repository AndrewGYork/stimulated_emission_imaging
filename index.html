We demonstrate experimental proof that stimulated emission is distinguishable from the stimulating beam. It has a point-like PSF (point spread function) and coherently interacts with the stimulating beam.

Introduction

Fluorescence is a powerful and widely-used tool in biological research. Fluorescent dyes and genetically-expressed fluorescent proteins help scientists determine the structure of organelles, track intra-cellular activity, quantify protein/antibody expression and binding, sequance genes, and measure concentration of chemicals. All of this is possible in spite of the fact that fluorescence signal is weak. Fluorescence is spontaneous emission of light from an electronically excited molecule (a fluorophore), and the rate of light emitted from the fluorophore is limited by the spontaneous decay rate of the excited molecule. The excited state lifetime for a fluorophore is on the order of ~1 ns. Luckily, fluorescent light can be imaged with acceptable contrast because its limited spectral bandwidth allows efficient filtering of optical noise sources like excitation light.

The slow spontaneous emission rate of fluorophores fundamentally limits our ability to quantify low-abundance cellular compounds, to view sub-cellular structures that are difficult to label, and to resolve fast sub-cellular events [examples, references]. Stimulated emission, on the other hand, is a radiative decay process that is initiated by incident light with optical frequency within the spectral bandwidth of the fluorescent emission. This process is instantaneous, and therefore can potentially be many orders of magnitude faster than spontaneous emission. Dark-field imaging of stimulated emission

We used dark-field imaging to attempt to image stimulated emission from N-v centers in nanodiamonds. The dark-field filter (a chrome disc in a back focal plane of our imaging system) is a high-pass filter in the Fourier domain. This means that it preferentially passes light with high spatial frequency (small PSF and features in the image plane) while attenuating transmitted light. Most of the stimulating beam does not interact with the nanodiamonds and is attenuated. The image light is comprised of stimulated emission light from the N-v centers (if stimulated emission has a point-like PSF), as well as the elastically scattered light from the stimulating beam incident on the nanodiamonds. Both of these signals are the same color as the stimulating beam, so they both pass through the narrow (10 nm wide) emission filter centered at 720 nm wavelength. Because diamond has a relatively high refractive index of 2.4 and the nanodiamonds are large, the scattered light is very bright and can easily saturate the dynamic range of the camera in less than one millisecond.

When the stimulating beam alone illuminates the sample, we simply observe the scattered light image of the nanodiamond. When both the excitation beam and the stimulating beam are incident upon the sample, we observe a change in brightness of the scattered light image of the nanodiamond, possibly due to stimulated emission. We show this observation in Figure 4. The scattered light images are taken with 24 simultaneous stimulation and excitation laser pulses, each at peak intensity with 1-μs duration, over a 15 ms camera exposure. The displayed image is an average of 200 such exposures. Stimulated emission occurs when the excitation laser pulses (peak excitation intensity) are incident simultaneously with the stimulation pulses.

The result in Figure 4 seems counterintuitive. Why would stimulated emission, which we hope to image in addition to the scattered light, make the scattered light image darker? We rule out N-v fluorescence light, since negligible fluorescence (less than one count) transmits through the narrow emission filter onto the camera with only the excitation beam on over the course of one exposure. Additionally, fluorescence due to the excitation pulse would incoherently add to the scattered light signal and appear as an increase in brightness of the image, as opposed to the decrease we observe.

We also rule out heating of the sample due to the excitation laser. In principle, sufficiently powerful excitation light could preferentially heat either the nanodiamond or the surrounding medium, causing a shape change that would subtly alter the amount of scattered light observed. Such a thermal effect on scattering should persist long (7 milliseconds?) after the microsecond duration excitation pulses [see supp mat for thermal effects]. In the control measurement where the stimulating pulse is delayed microseconds after the excitation pulse, the decrease in brightness of the scattered light disappears [supp mat or new figure?]. It is unlikely that the excitation pulse is thermally modulating the scattered light image.

If stimulated emission originates from the N-v centers distributed throughout the nanodiamonds, the PSF for both stimulated emission and scattered light should be identical. Since stimulated emission and scattering should coherently interfere, an axial scan of the brightness change of the dark-field scattered light should reveal differences, if any, between the PSF of the scattered light and the source of the brightness change. Note that the change in brightness of the scattered light from the nanodiamond varies axially in a similar manner as the brightness of the scattered light image. This suggests that the PSF of the signal is similar to that of elastically scattered light.

Phase contrast imaging of stimulated emission

Phase contrast imaging is a coherent imaging technique invented by Zernike [ref] that has better theoretical signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) than dark-field imaging. Because stimulated emission is coherent, phase contrast imaging is an ideal technique to boost SNR compared to dark-field imaging and simple fluorescence imaging. We adapted our dark-field imaging setup to perform phase contrast imaging. Our dark-field system contained a chrome disc used as a dark-field beam block in one of the back focal planes that we replaced with a chrome annulus that allowed a small fraction of transmitted light to evenly fill the field of view of the camera. We centered a phase mask (a laser-drilled hole in a (?mm thick) piece of glass) on another back focal plane of the imaging system. This mask gives a relative phase shift between image light, which passes through the glass, and transmitted light, which passes through the hole. For highest contrast imaging, we optimized two parameters: the relative phase (by carefully changing the angle of the phase mask), and the relative magnitude of the transmitted and image light (by choosing a chrome annulus with the correct inner and outer diameter). The experimental diagram is available in the online version of Figure 2 and also in the supplementary material.

Nanodiamonds doped with N-v centers are not an ideal candidate for phase contrast imaging of stimulated emission. In dark-field imaging of the nanodiamonds, the magnitude of the elastically scattered light dominates that of the signal we hope to be stimulated emission. The scattered light is coherent, so in phase contrast imaging, the magnitude of the interference term between the transmitted light background and the scattered light would still dominate that between transmitted light and stimulated emission. As with the dark-field results discussed earlier, any stimulated emission would be visible only as a small perturbation of the scattered light signal. Experimental data for this measurement is available in the supplemental material.

One way to minimize scattered light without decreasing stimulated emission is to match the refractive index of the mounting medium to that of the fluorescent objects. Index matching media are not readily available for diamond particles, but off-the-shelf matching media [meltmount reference] are available for lower-index imaging standards such as polystyrene beads (n ≈ 1.6). We used the above described phase contrast imaging system to empirically determine the fractional mixture of different mounting media to minimize scattered light from polystyrene beads infused with crimson fluorescent dye (please see supp mat for procedure). We then measured phase contrast images of the index-matched beads. In Figure 7, we show the image that appeared on top of the transmitted light background only when the N-v centers were excited. We tilted the phase plate to two angles that maximally enhanced the signal contrast, one of which caused the sign of the signal to reverse. The phase contrast images are taken with 5 simultaneous stimulation and excitation laser pulses, each at peak intensity with 1-μs duration, over a 4 ms camera exposure. The displayed images are an average of two camera exposures.

The signal we measured requires the excitation beam and it appears coherent: it can constructively and destructively interfere with the coherent transmitted light background. We would expect stimulated emission to also have these two characteristics.

Unlike N-v centers, the crimson fluorescent dye in the polystyrene beads photobleaches after prologed excitation. If the coherent emission we measured is stimulated emission, we would expect it to decrease as well after prolonged excitation. We performed this control measurement while keeping the phase plate at an angle that caused the signal to constructively interfere with the transmitted light background. Figure 8 shows this signal decreasing after long exposure to the excitation beam. The phase contrast images are taken with 5 simultaneous stimulation and excitation laser pulses, each at peak intensity with 1-μs duration, over a 4 ms camera exposure. Each point on the graph is the average number of counts per pixel in the main lobe of an image made by averaging 100 camera exposures.
