Abstract

We demonstrate experimental evidence that stimulated emission from a point-like emitter is distinguishable from the stimulating beam. The emission propagates spherically and coherently interferes with the stimulating beam. Imaging via stimulated emission could potentially combine the high signal rates of transmitted light microscopy with the specificity of fluorescence microscopy.

Introduction

Fluorescence is a widely-used tool in biological research. Fluorescent dyes and genetically-expressed fluorescent proteins help scientists determine the structure of organelles, track intra-cellular activity, quantify protein expression and distribution, sequence genes, and measure concentration of chemicals.

Fluorescence is the emission of light from an energetically excited material as it spontaneously decays to a lower energy state [Einstein 1917]. For a group of \(N\) fluorophores, the total emission rate is \(n_2N/\tau_{fluor}\), where \(n_2\) is the fraction of the population in the excited state and \(1/\tau_{fluor}\) is the spontaneous emission rate constant [Hell 1994]. The genetically expressed green fluorescent protein (GFP) and the Alexa Fluor 488 dye have \(\tau_{fluor}\) of 3.2 ns and 4.0 ns, respectively [Berezin 2010]. \(\tau_{fluor}\) depends on the environment (e.g. pH, temperature), but for typical fluorophores used in microscopy, \(\tau_{fluor}\) ranges from 0.5 ns to 5 ns [Thorn 2017] and is not easily tuned.

The accuracy of a fluorescence measurement improves as the amount of fluorescent emission increases [Pawley 2006]. What are the limits on fluorescent emission? \(n_2\) is controlled by excitation power, but cannot exceed 1. \(N\) can be increased via over-expression or multiple tagging, but this often causes undesired artifacts in biological samples. Increasing exposure time increases fluorescent signal but degrades time resolution. Therefore, a fixed \(\tau_{fluor}\) represents a practical limit to our ability to resolve fast sub-cellular dynamics, especially when quantifying low-abundance proteins or viewing structures whose function is impaired by excessive fluorescent tagging.

Stimulated emission, an alternative radiative decay process, is the emission of light from an energetically excited material, triggered by light applied to the material [Einstein 1917]. The rate of stimulated emission is \(h_{stim}\sigma_{23}n_2N\), where \(h_{stim}\) is the intensity of the stimulating light and \(\sigma_{23}\) is the cross-section for the optical transition [Hell 1994]. Unlike spontaneous emission, the stimulated emission rate constant \(h_{stim}\sigma_{23}\) is easily tuned over a large range by changing the stimulating light intensity \(h_{stim}\). A stimulation intensity of ~200 mW/µm2 results in \(h_{stim}\sigma_{23}\) roughly equal to \(1/\tau_{fluor}\) [Willig 2007], while pulsed stimulating light with similar average power yields \(h_{stim}\sigma_{23}\) three orders of magnitude larger than \(1/\tau_{fluor}\) [Dyba 2000].

A fixed spontaneous emission rate constant \(1/\tau_{fluor}\) limits the speed of fluorescent imaging. Given that the stimulated emission rate constant \(h_{stim}\sigma_{23}\) can be orders of magnitude higher, can we use stimulated emission to take images orders of magnitude faster than fluorescence images? In Figure 1a, a single fluorophore (small circle, left) is energetically excited by a pulse of green light. Next, a pulse of red light stimulates the fluorophore to emit this energy as red light. A small, opaque beam block (black rectangle, center) absorbs the stimulating pulse (Fig. 1a with Microscope type: Dark-field selected). Where will the stimulated emission go? Will any of it reach the camera? Will it form an image of the fluorophore on the camera? More generally, what is the shape in space and time of stimulated emission from an isolated, point-like emitter?

In their 1964 Nobel lectures on stimulated emission, Townes and Prokhorov remarked:

"... [stimulated emission] has the same field distribution and frequency as the stimulating radiation..." [Townes 1964]

"... stimulated emission occurs when an excited atom emits due to interaction with an external field (quantum). Then two quanta are involved: one is the external one, the other is emitted by the atom itself. Those two quanta are indistinguishable, i.e. their frequency and directivity coincide." [Prokhorov 1964]

Half a century later, this is still our understanding of stimulated emission:

"... in this process, called stimulated emission, a photon encountering an excited molecule produces a copy of itself, thus adding another photon of the same colour and propagation direction to the beam." [Hell 2009]

"An atom or molecule in its excited state can be stimulated down to the ground state by an incident light field, resulting in the creation of a new coherent photon identical to those in the original incident field." [Min 2009]

If stimulated emission has the same field distribution as the stimulating radiation, then perhaps the shape of the stimulated emission in space and time is identical to and indistinguishable from that of the stimulating beam, as illustrated in Figure 1b below.

In this model, the energy originating from the fluorophore is spread evenly over the stimulating pulse (indicated in Fig. 1b by a stronger red color). Like the stimulating pulse, the stimulated emission is absorbed by the beam block (Fig. 1b with Microscope type: Dark-field selected), and none of it reaches the camera. Even without the beam block, the stimulated emission would not form an image of the fluorophore on the camera, because the shape of the stimulated emission does not depend on the location of the fluorophore. In fact, this model predicts that no information about the fluorophore's position is encoded in the stimulated emission. Finally, note that if the field of the stimulating pulse is well-collimated (meaning it has a well-defined direction of propagation, and therefore flat phase fronts with a large lateral extent), then the same will be true of the field of the stimulated emission. It is not obvious how this emitted field obeys causality.

In Fig. 1b, when the center of the stimulating pulse contacts the excited fluorophore, the top and bottom edges of the stimulating pulse immediately become brighter. How can a point-like source instantaneously generate an electromagnetic field a large distance from its position?

"... an effect observed at the point \(\vec{x}\) at time \(t\) is caused by the action of a source a distance \(R\) away at an earlier or retarded time, \(t'=t-R/c\)." [Jackson 1998]

If stimulated emission occurs during the stimulating pulse duration \(\Delta t\), then the effects of stimulated emission must lie on an expanding spherical shell with outer/inner radius \(c(t \pm \Delta t/2)\), as illustrated in Fig. 1c below.

In this model, the energy originating from the fluorophore propagates spherically outwards. Unlike the stimulating pulse, some of the stimulated emission continues past the beam block and forms an image of the fluorophore on the camera (Fig. 1c with Microscope type: Dark-field selected). If an attenuating beam block is substituted for the opaque one (Fig. 1c with Microscope type: Phase contrast selected), the attenuated stimulating pulse forms a uniform background on the camera that coherently interferes with the stimulated emission. While this model describes stimulated emission that is causally related to its source, it is not obvious how the spherical stimulated emission can be considered to have the same field distribution [Townes 1964] as the collimated stimulating radiation.

The light path depicted in Fig. 1c potentially allows faster imaging via stimulated emission; the one in Fig. 1b does not. How shall we decide if either model accurately describes the shape in space and time of stimulated emission from an isolated, point-like emitter? Cray 1982 and Vahala 1993 theoretically explored a related question by modeling point-like stimulated emitters as classical oscillating dipoles. This reasonable assumption is consistent with the model in Fig. 1c, but to our knowledge, no experimental work has directly tested it.

Min and Dellwig proposed stimulated emission as an improved contrast mechanism compared to fluorescence, and they imaged fluorophores in biological samples via stimulated emission [Min 2009, Dellwig 2012]. They assumed that stimulated emission is indistinguishable from the stimulating beam (consistent with the model in Fig. 1b.) and used single-pixel detection to measure the change in intensity of the stimulating beam due to stimulated emission. Unfortunately, such measurements do not reveal the shape of stimulated emission, making it difficult to rule out Fig. 1b or 1c.

Gather and Chen used fluorescently labeled cells as the gain medium of an optically excited laser cavity, and imaged the stimulated emission onto a multi-pixel detector [Gather 2011, Chen 2017]. Their stimulating beam shape was determined by a complicated combination of cavity feedback, sample refractive index, and fluorophore distribution. Since they simultaneously measured the stimulating beam and the stimulated emission, but did not separately characterize their shapes, it is similarly difficult to rule out Fig. 1b or 1c based on these measurements.

Since we were unable to find existing literature that unambiguously predicts what the detector in Figure 1a would see, we performed the experiment. We imaged stimulated emission from nitrogen-vacancy (N-v) centers in nanodiamonds and from fluorescent polystyrene beads, in a setup similar to the one depicted in Figure 1a. We used dark-field microscopy (Figure 1a with Microscope type: Dark-field selected) and phase contrast microscopy (Figure 1a with Microscope type: Phase contrast selected). Our results are consistent with Figure 1c's prediction, suggesting that stimulated emission from a point-like source propagates spherically and is distinguishable from the stimulation beam.

Experimental design

describe illuminationFigure 2 shows a photograph and schematic of our experimental setup. The sample is illuminated by two continuous wave lasers (532 nm wavelength for excitation and 721 nm for stimulation), each gated to ~1.25 µs pulse duration via acousto-optic modulators. For convenience, we chose to deliver the excitation beam parallel to the stimulation beam, rather than perpendicular as in Figure 1a. The sample is positioned at the stimulation beam waist, and the excitation and stimulation beams illuminate a ~10 µm diameter region of the sample. The peak excitation intensity is ~15 mW/µm2 and the peak stimulation intensity is ~3 mW/µm2.

describe collectionThree lens pairs in the collection path give 750X total magnification, spreading light from a point-like source in the sample plane across ~103 camera pixels, and yielding a single-particle intensity dynamic range greater than 107. The ~10 µm stimulation beam waist is 36 times wider than the ~280 nm lateral resolution of the collection path. The stimulation beam fills ~1/36 of the numerical aperture (NA) of the collection objective lens and illuminates less than 10-3 of the area of the objective's back focal plane, whereas light emitted from a point-like source in the sample plane fills the entire NA and illuminates the entire back focal plane. This minimal spatial overlap allows us to manipulate the stimulation beam at the back focal plane with little effect on emission or scatter from point-like particles in the sample (as in Figure 1c).

spatial filtersThe collection objective's back focal plane is inconveniently located inside the collection objective, but the collection path lenses create two accessible images of the back focal plane where we place spatial filters. For dark-field imaging, we use a small chrome disc to block the stimulation beam (Figure 2b with Imaging method: Dark-field... selected). For phase contrast imaging, we use a small chrome annulus to attenuate the stimulation beam, and a drilled glass slide to apply a relative phase shift between the low-NA stimulation beam and high-NA light emitted from point-like sources in the sample (Figure 2b with Imaging method: Phase contrast... selected). Rotating the glass slide adjusts the phase shift, and substituting annuli with different inner diameters adjusts the attenuation. Note that a small amount of the light from point-like sources passes through the hole in the phase plate, giving an approximate (rather than pure) phase shift. However, the amount is quite small (<0.1%) so the approximation is quite good.

Results

Phase contrast imaging of stimulated emission

what do we expect? We expect stimulated emission (unlike fluorescence) to coherently interfere with the stimulating beam. Phase contrast microscopy of index-matched beads allows us to test that expectation (Figure 2b with Imaging method: Phase contrast imaging of stimulated emission... and Fluorescent sample type: Beads... selected). Because index matching minimizes scattering, this closely resembles our thought experiment (Figure 1a with Microscope type: Phase contrast selected), where the stimulating beam provides a bright, uniform, coherent background for the stimulated emission to interfere with.

If the model in Figure 1b is accurate, we expect this background to become (slightly) uniformly brighter when the bead is excited, due to stimulated emission. Tuning the angle of the phase plate would not change the camera signal, because the stimulated emission would overlap perfectly with the stimulation beam, which does not contact the plate.

If the model in Figure 1c is accurate, we expect stimulated emission to form an image of the excited bead on the camera, and we expect this image to coherently interfere with the stimulation beam background. Rotating the phase plate would apply a phase shift to the stimulated emission, tuning the magnitude and sign of the interference. Additionally, tuning the amount of scattered light (by slightly mismatching the refractive index of the bead's mounting medium) would allow us to estimate the relative phase between transmitted, scattered, and stimulated light.

what did we do? We prepared three bead samples using mounting media with refractive index lower than the beads \((\Delta n\approx 0.05)\), the same as the beads \((\Delta n\approx 0)\), and higher than the beads \((\Delta n\approx -0.01)\). For each sample, we rotated the phase plate to fifteen evenly-spaced angles, spanning one complete wavelength of phase shift. At each angle, we took three exposures with different excitation pulse delays, yielding phase contrast images of beads 2.5 µs before excitation, during excitation, and 2.5 µs after excitation. For each exposure, we used 5 maximum-intensity excitation/stimulation pulse pairs with 1.25 µs duration and 600 µs between pairs.

Fluorescence and stimulated emission depletion

what are our expectations?Fluorophore vibrational relaxation rate constants \(1/\tau_{vibr}\) dominate their spontaneous emission rate constants \(1/\tau_{fluor}\), and given our modest laser intensities, they also dominate excitation and stimulated emission rate constants \(h_{exc}\sigma_{01}\) and \(h_{stim}\sigma_{23}\). In this limit, the model from [Hell 1994] reduces to:

$$\begin{equation} \frac{dn_{2}}{dt} = h_{exc}\sigma_{01}(1-n_2) - h_{stim}\sigma_{23}n_2 - \frac{1}{\tau_{fluor}}n_2 \end{equation}$$

We'd like to confirm that our fluorescent signals are consistent with this model. We'd also like to estimate the proportionality between measured excitation and stimulation power (in milliwatts) and the transition rate constants \(h_{stim}\sigma_{23}\) and \(h_{exc}\sigma_{01}\), as well as the proportionality between measured fluorescence (in camera counts per second) and the fluorescence emission rate \(n_2 N/\tau_{fluor}\).

what did we do?We imaged our fluorophores as illustrated in Figure 2b with Imaging method: Fluorescence depletion imaging selected. We exposed the samples to a train of simultaneous excitation and stimulation pulses over a single camera exposure, with 600 µs between consecutive pulse pairs. We used 700 pulse pairs per exposure for the nanodiamonds, and 5 for the beads. We tuned the peak excitation power from 0 to ~1.4 W for stimulation powers of 0 and 300 mW while measuring fluorescence (Figure 3). Each point on the plot indicates the average brightness of the pixels in the central lobe of the fluorescence image (selected by a square with side length ~ 450 nm, Figure 3 inset). did our observations confirm our expectations?At equilibrium, \(\frac{dn_{2}}{dt} = 0\), yielding:

$$\begin{equation} n_2 = \frac{h_{exc}\sigma_{01}}{h_{exc}\sigma_{01} + h_{stim}\sigma_{23} + \frac{1}{\tau_{fluor}}} \end{equation}$$

When we fit the data to our model, we estimated the maximum excitation rate constant \(h_{exc}\sigma_{01}\approx1.4(1/\tau_{fluor})\) and excitation fraction \(n_2\approx0.6\) for both the N-v doped nanodiamonds and the crimson beads. At the maximum available excitation beam and stimulation beam powers, we observed ~8% depletion for both N-v and crimson dye fluorescence and estimated the stimulation rate constant \(h_{stim}\sigma_{23}\approx0.3(1/\tau_{fluor})\). The fit to experimental data is not perfect; others have measured a similar N-v fluorescence saturation shape [Geiselmann 2013], and bleaching of the crimson dye caused difficulty in repeatably measuring fluorescence from a single bead. The main inferences we draw from our measurement and fit are (1), that at maximum excitation beam power, excitation approaches saturation, and (2), that the depletion of fluorescence resembles the depletion we would expect from stimulated emission. Dark-field imaging of stimulated emission

why not phase contrastIn phase contrast imaging [Zernike 1942], transmitted light is allowed to interfere with scattered light from the image object, unlike dark-field imaging, which solely minimizes transmitted light. When the relative phase between transmitted and scattered light is tuned to maximize their interference, the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of the scattered light is limited only by the dynamic range of the detector. Because stimulated emission, like scattered light, is coherent, phase contrast imaging could be an ideal technique to explore the coherence of the signal we measured in the dark-field imaging scheme. We adapted our setup to attempt phase contrast imaging as described in the experimental design section.

nanodiamonds not idealNanodiamonds doped with N-v centers are not an ideal candidate for phase contrast imaging of stimulated emission. In dark-field imaging of the nanodiamonds, the magnitude of the elastically scattered light dominates that of the signal we hope to be stimulated emission by a factor of ~500:1. The scattered light is coherent, so in phase contrast imaging, the magnitude of the interference term between the transmitted light background and the scattered light would still dominate that between transmitted light and stimulated emission. As with the dark-field results discussed earlier, any stimulated emission would be visible only as a small perturbation of the scattered light signal. We experimentally confirmed this hypothesis, and have made the results available in the appendix.

what are our expectationsWe used dark-field imaging to attempt to image stimulated emission from N-v centers in nanodiamonds. Dark-field imaging is a technique commonly used to separate high spatial frequency light from wide-field illumination in order to enhance the signal contrast of scattered light. If stimulated emission from a point-like particle propagates spherically from its source, then the spatial frequency bandwidth of the emission should be greater than that of the incident stimulation electromagnetic field, and the two fields should be separable in the back focal plane of the imaging system as long as the stimulation beam is not confined to a diffraction-limited focus of the imaging system. The dark-field filter (an opaque disc in a back focal plane of our imaging system) is a high-pass filter in the Fourier domain. This means that it preferentially passes light with high spatial frequency (i.e. small features in the image plane) while attenuating transmitted light. Most of the stimulating beam does not interact with the nanodiamonds and is blocked. The light that passes around the dark-field filter is comprised of the elastically scattered light from the stimulating beam incident on the nanodiamonds, and possibly stimulated emission light from the N-v centers. Both of these signals are the same color as the stimulating beam, so they both pass through the narrow (10 nm wide) emission filter centered at 720 nm wavelength. Because diamond has a relatively high refractive index of 2.42 and the nanodiamonds are large, the scattered light is very bright and can fill the dynamic range of the camera in less than 40 µs.

what did we do

do the results match our expectationsWhen the stimulating beam alone illuminated the sample, we solely observed the scattered light image of the nanodiamond. When both the excitation beam and the stimulating beam were incident upon the sample, we observed a decrease in brightness of the scattered light image of the nanodiamond, possibly due to stimulated emission. We show this observation in Figure 4. The scattered light images are taken with a train of 24 simultaneous stimulation and excitation laser pulses, each at peak intensity with 1 μs duration, over a 15 ms camera exposure. The displayed image is an average of 200 such exposures. The decrease in brightness of the scattered light image occurs only when the excitation laser pulses are incident simultaneously with the stimulation pulses (and not before or after).

wtf decrease in signalThe result in Figure 4 seems counterintuitive. Why would stimulated emission, which we hope to image in addition to the scattered light, make the scattered light image darker? This could happen if the scattered electric field destructively interferes with the stimulated emission electric field. Interference is possible because both stimulated emission and scattered light are coherent with the incident electric field, and therefore have a distinct phase relationship with each other.

why we expect destructive interferenceTo predict this phase relationship, we start with incident electric field \(E_0e^{i\omega t + \phi_0}\), stimulated emission field \(E_{stim}e^{i\omega t + \phi_{stim}}\), and scattered field \(E_{scat}e^{i\omega t + \phi_{scat}}\). Because stimulated emission is considered a nearly instantaneous process [Townes 1964], there is no delay between the incident electric field and the stimulated emission electric field:

$$\begin{equation}\phi_{stim} = \phi_0 \end{equation}$$
We also know elastically scattered light has a minimum \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) phase delay with respect to the incident electric field [Zernike 1955], and larger delay for light scattered by particles that do not have a sufficiently small diameter for the Rayleigh approximation \(d\ll \frac{\lambda}{\pi}\), so:
$$\begin{equation}\phi_{scat} - \phi_0 \ge \frac{\pi}{2} \end{equation}$$
Our diameter \(d\sim 80\,nm\) nanodiamonds are slightly larger than the small diameter approximation allows for \(\lambda=721\,nm\). To estimate the additional refractive phase shift, we note that the refractive index of diamond \(n_{diamond}=2.42\), while the mounting medium \(n_{oil}=1.52\), with a difference \(\Delta n_{refractive}=0.9\). The approximate phase difference due to different refractive optical path lengths is:
$$\begin{equation}\Delta\phi_{refractive} \sim \frac{2\pi d\Delta n_{refractive}}{\lambda} \approx 0.6 \end{equation}$$
We can then estimate the relative phase between scattered light and stimulated emission:
$$\begin{equation}\Delta\phi = \phi_{scat} - \phi_{stim} \sim \frac{\pi}{2} + \Delta\phi_{refractive} \approx 2.2 \end{equation}$$

This fulfills the requirement for destructive interference:

$$\begin{equation} \frac{\pi}{2} \lt \Delta\phi \lt \frac{3\pi}{2} \end{equation}$$

no heatingWe also ruled out heating of the sample due to the excitation laser. Sufficiently energetic excitation laser pulses could preferentially heat either the nanodiamond or the surrounding medium, causing a shape change that would subtly alter the amount of scattered light observed. Such a thermal effect on scattering would persist long after the microsecond duration excitation pulses. In the control measurement where the stimulating pulse is delayed microseconds after the excitation pulse, the decrease in brightness of the scattered light disappears. These results are available in the appendix. In order to probe the regime in which the excitation laser heats the sample, we increased the excitation pulse duration to 20 microseconds and were able to detect a scattered light image change that peaked 250 µs after the end of the excitation pulse and decayed with a time constant of ~ 1 ms (see the appendix for this data). This result resembles a thermal effect because of its persistance and dissipation well after the end of the laser pulse. Time-delayed effects did not appear in any of the images we took while attempting to image stimulated emission.

The stimulated emission rate should depend on stimulation intensity \(h_{stim}\) and excitation fraction \(n_2\), the latter of which depends on excitation intensity (Note that our fluorescence measurements indicate neither excitation nor stimulated emission depletion are fully saturated.). Figure 5 shows the change in brightness of the main scattered lobe for increasing excitation and stimulation beam peak power. For this power scan, the image acquisition and averaging parameters are the same as that for the data in Figure 3. Discussion

seems like stim emissionThe point-like signal we imaged demonstrates many of the hallmarks of stimulated emission: it interferes coherently with transmitted and scattered light, it seems instantaneous (the time response is 1 µs or shorter), it depends on excitation and stimulation intensity, and its brightness decreases as the fluorescent source bleaches.

plan to beat spont emissionWe have yet to demonstrate an enhanced signal rate as compared with spontaneous emission due to lack of intensity in our stimulating laser beam. We are currently building a new imaging system using higher power sub-nanosecond duration excitation and stimulation laser pulses. This system should allow us to confirm that the stimulated emission rate will outcompete the spontaneous emission rate for excitation/stimulation pulse delays that span the spontaneous emission lifetime.

speculate on benefits of higher emission rateThere are many imaging and detection applications that could significantly benefit from the high signal rate and coherent detection capability made possible by stimulated emission. Stimulated emission may make it easier to track fast sub-cellular processes such as electrochemical impulses. These transients are currently only resolvable through the use of voltage-sensitive dyes, which have notoriously dim fluorescence. It may also become easier to visualize and quantify low-abundance proteins (e.g. transcription factors) and cellular structures tagged with dim fluorophores. While fluorophore brightness may not be the major bottleneck in next-generation genetic sequencing speed or reliability, sequencing could still benefit from the ability to squeeze more signal from fluorophores.

flow cytometry + imagingNo saturation limit on emission would greatly aid measuring fluorophores in flow cytometry devices, which usually have only microseconds to collect as much signal as possible. Additionally, coherently detected signals do not require low read noise detectors, so one could imagine replacing the fast, low noise, point detectors currently part of flow cytometry devices with array detectors that are just as fast. Flow cytometry that outputs images instead of one-dimensional signals would break the Poisson loading limit while also measuring valuable multi-dimensional information (e.g. aggregation).

minimize bleachingBecause stimulated emission can be extracted from an excited fluorophore in less time than spontaneous emission, the fluorophore would spend less time in the excited state for the same amount of optical output. Photobleaching, in some cases, is thought to depend on the amount of time spent in the excited state [Li 2015]. Stimulated emission imaging could significantly enhance the signal available from fluorophores subject to photobleaching due to inter-molecular collisions while in the excited state.
